[TOPIC: The Serve]

The serve in pickleball is the shot that initiates every rally, and mastering it is essential for controlling the pace and direction of the game. Unlike tennis, the serve in pickleball is underhand and must be executed with the paddle below the waist at the point of contact. This makes the serve less about power and more about placement, spin, and consistency.

The basic serve technique starts with the player standing behind the baseline with both feet firmly planted. The ball is held in the non-paddle hand and dropped in front of the body. As the ball drops, the player swings the paddle in an upward arc, contacting the ball below waist level. The motion should be smooth and natural, similar to a pendulum swing. The follow-through should be toward the target, with the paddle finishing above shoulder height.

There are several types of serves that players can use to vary their attack. The power serve is hit with maximum force, aimed deep in the opponent's service court. This serve is effective for pushing the receiver back and making it harder for them to hit a strong return. However, power serves are less consistent and can lead to more faults.

The placement serve focuses on accuracy rather than power. The goal is to hit the ball to a specific spot in the service court, such as the backhand corner, the sideline, or the center of the court. Placement serves are more consistent than power serves and can be just as effective if they target the receiver's weaknesses.

Spin adds another dimension to the serve. Topspin is generated by brushing up on the ball, causing it to dip faster and bounce higher. Sidespin is generated by brushing across the ball, causing it to curve in the air and bounce at an angle. Backspin is generated by brushing down on the ball, causing it to float and bounce lower. Each type of spin can be effective depending on the situation and the receiver's ability to handle spin.

The lob serve is a high, arcing shot that lands deep in the opponent's service court. This serve is effective for changing the pace and catching the receiver off guard, especially if they are expecting a flat or spinning serve. The lob serve gives the server more time to approach the net and can be particularly useful against aggressive receivers.

Serve strategy is about more than just hitting the ball into the correct court. The server should consider the receiver's position, strengths, and weaknesses. For example, if the receiver has a weak backhand, the server should target that side. If the receiver is standing far back, a short serve might be effective. If the receiver is aggressive and rushing the net, a deep lob serve can push them back and disrupt their timing.

Consistency is the most important aspect of the serve. Since there is no second serve in pickleball, a fault results in a loss of serve. The server should practice hitting reliable serves that land in the correct court with minimal risk of fault. Once consistency is achieved, the server can gradually add more power, spin, and placement to their serves.

[TOPIC: The Return of Serve]

The return of serve is the second shot in a pickleball rally, and it is a critical opportunity for the receiving team to gain an advantage. A strong return can put pressure on the serving team and set up the receiving team for a successful rally.

The primary goal of the return of serve is to hit the ball deep into the opponent's court. A deep return pushes the serving team back toward the baseline, making it harder for them to approach the net. It also gives the receiving team more time to advance to the kitchen line and establish a strong position. The ideal return lands within a few feet of the baseline, forcing the serving team to hit their third shot from a deep position.

The return should be hit after the ball bounces, as required by the two-bounce rule. The receiver should position themselves behind the baseline and take a balanced stance. As the ball approaches, the receiver should step forward and hit the ball with a smooth, controlled swing. The goal is to hit the ball firmly enough to reach the back of the opponent's court, but not so hard that it goes out of bounds.

Targeting the weaker player is a key strategy in doubles. If one opponent has a weaker backhand or is less mobile, the return should be directed toward that player. This can force errors and create opportunities for the receiving team to win the point.

After hitting the return, the receiving team should immediately advance to the kitchen line. This is one of the biggest advantages of the return: the receiving team can approach the net after hitting the return, while the serving team must wait for the return to bounce before they can hit their third shot. This gives the receiving team a positional advantage if they can reach the kitchen line quickly.

The return can also be varied with spin and placement. A topspin return dips faster and bounces higher, making it harder for the opponent to handle. A slice return stays low and can force the opponent to hit up on the ball. A cross-court return creates a longer angle and can catch the opponent off guard.

In doubles, the return should be directed toward the server or the middle of the court to minimize the opponent's ability to poach. A return down the middle is generally safer and more effective than a return down the line, as it reduces the angle for the opponent's third shot.

[TOPIC: Dinking]

Dinking is the soft game at the kitchen line, and it is one of the most important and nuanced aspects of pickleball. Dinking involves hitting soft shots that land in the opponent's kitchen, forcing them to hit up on the ball and creating opportunities to attack.

The basic dink is a gentle shot that just clears the net and lands in the kitchen. The player should use a continental grip and a short, controlled swing, contacting the ball with a slight upward motion. The goal is to hit the ball with minimal force, allowing it to drop into the kitchen with a low bounce. The follow-through should be short and controlled, with the paddle finishing at about waist height.

Cross-court dinking is a variation where the ball is hit diagonally across the court. Cross-court dinks are effective because they create longer angles and give the dinking team more time to react. They also force the opponent to cover more ground and can create openings for attack.

Dink placement strategy is about hitting the ball to the opponent's weaknesses. If the opponent has a weak backhand, the dink should be directed to that side. If the opponent is slow to move, the dink should be placed to force them to cover the most distance. The goal is to create an opportunity to attack while minimizing the risk of making an error.

A speed-up from the dink is when a player suddenly hits the ball harder after a series of soft dinks. This can catch the opponent off guard and win the point outright. The speed-up should be hit when the opponent is off balance or when the ball is in a position that allows for a strong attack. The key is to disguise the speed-up as a dink, so the opponent does not anticipate the change in pace.

Dink battles can last for many shots, as both teams try to create an opportunity to attack. Patience is essential in dink battles, as the first team to make an error or hit a ball too high usually loses the point. The best players are comfortable in long dink rallies and can wait for the right moment to attack.

Dinking also involves reading the opponent's shots and anticipating their next move. If the opponent hits a dink that is too high, it is an opportunity to attack. If the opponent is moving forward, a dink to the side can catch them off balance. The best dinkers are always thinking one step ahead and using their dinks to set up attacks.

[TOPIC: Volleying]

Volleying is hitting the ball out of the air before it bounces, and it is a key skill for controlling the kitchen line. Effective volleying allows players to stay on the offensive and put pressure on the opponent.

The punch volley is the most common volley technique. It is a short, punchy motion where the player pushes the ball with the paddle, using a firm wrist and a short swing. The punch volley is effective for hitting the ball to a specific spot, such as the opponent's feet or the open court. The key is to keep the paddle in front of the body and use a compact motion.

The block volley is used for hard shots that are hit directly at the player. Instead of swinging at the ball, the player simply puts the paddle in front of the ball and lets the ball bounce off the paddle. The block volley is a defensive shot that neutralizes the opponent's power and allows the player to reset the rally.

Volley placement is critical for winning points at the net. The best placements are at the opponent's feet, which forces them to hit up on the ball. Other effective placements include the open court, the backhand side, and the body. The key is to place the ball where the opponent cannot reach it or where they are forced to hit a weak shot.

The reset volley is a defensive shot used when the player is under pressure. Instead of trying to hit a winner, the player hits a soft volley that lands in the opponent's kitchen, allowing them to regain their position. The reset volley requires soft hands and a controlled touch, similar to a dink.

Volleying also involves quick reflexes and good hand-eye coordination. The best volleyers can react to fast shots and place the ball accurately. They also know when to be aggressive and when to be defensive, based on the situation.
