[TOPIC: Basic Position Play]

Cue ball control in 3-cushion billiards (bida 3 bang) is the foundation of every successful shot. Unlike pool, where the goal is to pocket balls, 3-cushion requires the player to orchestrate a precise path for the cue ball that contacts at least three cushions before striking the second object ball. This means that controlling where the cue ball goes AFTER the initial contact is just as important as making the contact itself.

The first concept a beginner must understand is the relationship between the cue ball and the first object ball. In 3-cushion, the cue ball first contacts one of the two object balls (either the red or the opponent's cue ball), and then must travel to at least three cushion surfaces before contacting the second object ball. The angle at which the cue ball deflects off the first object ball is called the "natural angle" — it is determined by the contact point between the cue ball and the object ball.

Natural angle patterns in 3-cushion can be categorized into several families. The "thick" hit (where the cue ball contacts a large portion of the object ball) produces a narrow deflection angle, sending the cue ball nearly straight ahead. The "thin" hit (where the cue ball barely clips the object ball) produces a wide deflection angle, sending the cue ball far to the side. A half-ball hit (the most common reference point) deflects the cue ball at approximately 30 degrees from the original line of aim. Understanding these natural angles is critical because the player must predict where the cue ball will go after contact and plan the cushion contacts accordingly.

Speed control is equally important in 3-cushion. The speed of the cue ball affects how it rebounds off cushions — a faster shot produces a narrower rebound angle due to the compression of the cushion rubber, while a slower shot produces a wider rebound angle. In general, medium speed is the most predictable and is used for most diamond system shots. Players should practice controlling speed through their stroke rather than through backswing length. A smooth, consistent acceleration through the ball produces the most reliable speed control.

Stop shot, follow shot, and draw shot in carom context differ from their pool equivalents because there are no pockets to aim for. A stop shot (where the cue ball stops dead after contacting the object ball) is used when the player wants the cue ball to remain near the point of first contact before deflecting to the cushions. A follow shot (where the cue ball rolls forward after contact) is used to extend the cue ball's path, sending it deeper into the table and potentially contacting more cushions. A draw shot (where the cue ball pulls back after contact) is used to reverse the cue ball's direction, sending it back toward the head end of the table.

In 3-cushion, the follow and draw strokes are often used in combination with the diamond system to adjust the cue ball's path after hitting the first object ball. For example, a slight follow on the cue ball can change the deflection angle by 1-2 diamonds, which can be the difference between a successful three-cushion contact and a miss. Players should develop a feel for how much follow or draw changes the cue ball's trajectory and practice incorporating spin into their diamond system calculations.

[TOPIC: The Diamond System]

The diamond system is a mathematical aiming method used in 3-cushion billiards to calculate precise angles for multi-cushion shots. It uses the diamond markers (also called "pins" or "spots") on the rails as reference points. Each diamond is assigned a number, and the system uses simple addition to determine where to aim the cue ball. The diamond system is the most important technical concept in 3-cushion billiards and is essential for any serious player.

Diamond markers are positioned at regular intervals along the rails of the table. On a standard 10-foot table, there are 6 diamonds on each short rail (including the corner diamonds) and 9 diamonds on each long rail (including the corner diamonds). The diamonds are numbered differently depending on whether you are calculating from a short rail or a long rail. For the "plus 2" system (the most common), the diamonds on the long rails are numbered 1 through 7 (starting from the short rail), and the diamonds on the short rails are numbered 1 through 3 (starting from the long rail).

The "clock system" is a related concept where the diamonds are numbered to help players visualize the path of the cue ball. In this system, the numbers represent the position along the rail, and the player can use these numbers to calculate where the cue ball will end up after contacting three cushions. The clock system is particularly useful for visualizing the cue ball's path in real-time, allowing the player to make quick adjustments during play.

Short rail calculations (cross-table) involve the cue ball traveling from one short rail to the other, contacting a long rail in between. These shots are common when the first object ball is near one short rail and the second object ball is near the other. The calculation is similar to the long rail calculation, but the numbers are different because the short rail diamonds are numbered differently.

Long rail calculations are the most common in 3-cushion. The cue ball starts near one long rail, contacts the first object ball, and then travels to the opposite long rail (via one or more cushions). The "plus 2" formula applies: the starting diamond number plus the ending diamond number equals 2. For example, if the cue ball starts at diamond 3 on the long rail, it should end at diamond (2-3) = -1, which wraps around to diamond 1 on the opposite rail. This is a common pattern that players use to plan their shots.

The "plus 3" system (also called the "short" system) is used for shots with less rail travel. In this system, the starting diamond plus the ending diamond equals 3. The plus 3 system is used when the cue ball needs to travel a shorter distance across the table, typically when the first and second object balls are closer together. The plus 3 system is less common than the plus 2 system but is still an important tool for the player's arsenal.

Common diamond system shots include the "natural" (a shot where the cue ball contacts three cushions in a predictable pattern), the "cross-table" (where the cue ball travels from one short rail to the other), and the "end-rail" (where the cue ball contacts the short rail at the end of the table). Each of these shots can be calculated using the diamond system, and players should practice each one until they become second nature.

Limitations of the diamond system include: it does not account for spin on the cue ball (which can significantly change the rebound angle), it does not account for speed (faster shots produce narrower rebound angles), and it does not account for cloth conditions (new cloth is faster than old cloth). Experienced players learn to adjust the system based on these factors, developing a "feel" that allows them to make fine adjustments without relying solely on the math.

[TOPIC: Plus 2 System (Natural)]

The plus 2 system, also known as the "natural" system, is the most fundamental diamond system pattern in 3-cushion billiards. The formula is simple: the starting diamond number (where the cue ball begins its cushion journey) plus the ending diamond number (where the cue ball arrives at the second object ball) equals 2. This pattern works for the most common shot lengths in 3-cushion and is the first system every player should learn.

To calculate a plus 2 shot, the player must first identify the starting diamond. This is the diamond on the long rail that is closest to the point where the cue ball will contact the first cushion after deflecting off the first object ball. The ending diamond is the diamond on the opposite long rail that is closest to the position of the second object ball. The sum of these two numbers must equal 2.

Example 1: Start at diamond 1, end at diamond 1 (1+1=2). The cue ball starts near the corner of the table, travels to the short rail, then to the opposite long rail, and arrives at the second object ball near the same corner on the other side. This is a common pattern when both object balls are near the same end of the table.

Example 2: Start at diamond 2, end at diamond 0 (2+0=2). The cue ball starts at diamond 2 on the long rail, contacts the short rail, and returns to the long rail at the corner (diamond 0). This is a shorter pattern that is often used when the first object ball is near the middle of the table.

Example 3: Start at diamond 3, end at diamond -1 (3-1=2). The cue ball starts at diamond 3, contacts the short rail, and arrives at the opposite corner. This is a longer pattern that requires more speed and a wider angle.

Example 4: Start at diamond 0, end at diamond 2 (0+2=2). This is the reverse of Example 2. The cue ball starts at the corner and travels to diamond 2 on the opposite rail. This is a common pattern when the first object ball is near the corner and the second object ball is near the middle of the table.

Example 5: Start at diamond 1.5, end at diamond 0.5 (1.5+0.5=2). This is an intermediate pattern where the cue ball starts between diamonds and ends between diamonds. These intermediate patterns require more precise aiming and are often used in combination with spin or speed adjustments.

Rail contacts in the plus 2 system typically follow a pattern: the cue ball contacts the short rail first, then the opposite long rail, and finally the second object ball. However, the exact path depends on the starting and ending positions. In some cases, the cue ball may contact the long rail first, then the short rail, and then the other long rail. The player must visualize the path and adjust the starting position accordingly.

Practice drills for the plus 2 system should start with simple patterns and gradually increase in complexity. Begin with the most common patterns (1+1=2 and 2+0=2) and practice until you can consistently score. Then move on to more complex patterns (3-1=2 and intermediate values). Finally, practice combining the plus 2 system with spin and speed adjustments to handle real-game situations.

[TOPIC: Plus 3 System (Short)]

The plus 3 system is used for shorter patterns where the cue ball travels less distance across the table. The formula is: starting diamond number plus ending diamond number equals 3. This system is typically used when the first and second object balls are closer together, or when the cue ball needs to take a more direct path to the second object ball.

The plus 3 system is less common than the plus 2 system but is still an essential tool for the player's arsenal. It is particularly useful in situations where the plus 2 system would send the cue ball too far across the table, or where the player needs to control the speed more precisely.

Example 1: Start at diamond 1, end at diamond 2 (1+2=3). The cue ball starts near the corner, contacts the short rail, and arrives at diamond 2 on the opposite long rail. This is a common pattern when the first object ball is near the corner and the second object ball is near the middle of the table.

Example 2: Start at diamond 2, end at diamond 1 (2+1=3). This is the reverse of Example 1. The cue ball starts at diamond 2 and arrives near the corner on the opposite side. This pattern is often used when the first object ball is near the middle and the second object ball is near the corner.

Example 3: Start at diamond 3, end at diamond 0 (3+0=3). The cue ball starts at diamond 3, contacts the short rail, and arrives at the corner on the opposite side. This is a longer pattern that requires more speed and precision.

Example 4: Start at diamond 0.5, end at diamond 2.5 (0.5+2.5=3). This is an intermediate pattern where the cue ball starts near the corner and arrives between diamonds 2 and 3 on the opposite rail. These intermediate patterns are common in real-game situations.

Example 5: Start at diamond 1.5, end at diamond 1.5 (1.5+1.5=3). This is a symmetrical pattern where the cue ball starts and ends at the same relative position on opposite rails. This pattern is often used when both object balls are in similar positions on opposite sides of the table.

The plus 3 system is particularly useful for "short" patterns where the cue ball needs to travel a shorter distance. In these situations, the plus 2 system would send the cue ball too far, and the player would need to use excessive speed to compensate. The plus 3 system provides a more natural path that requires less speed and is easier to control.

When to use plus 3 vs plus 2: The plus 3 system is preferred when the cue ball's path is more direct (fewer extreme angles), when the object balls are closer together, and when the player needs to control the speed more precisely. The plus 2 system is preferred when the cue ball needs to travel a longer distance, when the angles are more extreme, and when the player wants to use a more predictable pattern.

[TOPIC: Masse and Curve Shots]

Masse shots in 3-cushion billiards are used when the cue ball needs to travel around an obstructing object ball or when the natural angle from the diamond system is not achievable due to ball positions. A masse shot involves striking the cue ball with an elevated cue (often at 45 degrees or more) and applying extreme spin to curve the cue ball's path. Masse shots are among the most difficult techniques in billiards and require significant practice to master.

Technique for masse shots: The player elevates the butt of the cue stick to an angle of 30-70 degrees from the horizontal. The cue tip strikes the cue ball low and to one side, applying both backspin and sidespin. The combination of the elevated angle and the spin causes the cue ball to curve on the cloth. The amount of curve depends on the elevation angle, the amount of spin, and the speed of the stroke. A higher elevation and more spin produce a tighter curve; a lower elevation and less spin produce a wider curve.

Common masse patterns in 3-cushion game situations include: (1) Curving around an obstructing object ball to reach the first target ball, (2) Using a masse to change the direction of the cue ball after contacting the first object ball, (3) Combining a masse with the diamond system to achieve a three-cushion contact that would not be possible with a straight shot.

Safety considerations for masse shots include: (1) Always ensure the cue tip is properly chalked to prevent miscues, (2) Use a firm, controlled stroke rather than a wild swing, (3) Keep your bridge hand stable and close to the cue ball, (4) Be aware that masse shots can damage the cloth if the cue tip strikes the cloth instead of the ball, (5) Practice masse shots on a dedicated practice table before attempting them in a game situation.

The "jenny" shot is a variation of the masse that is commonly used in 3-cushion. In a jenny, the cue ball is struck with a slight elevation and extreme sidespin, causing it to curve into the first object ball and then continue curving to contact the cushions. The jenny is often used when the cue ball and the first object ball are close together and the player needs to send the cue ball on a curved path to the second object ball.

[TOPIC: Strategy and Defense]

Defensive play in 3-cushion billiards is just as important as offensive play. Unlike pool, where the goal is to pocket balls, 3-cushion requires the cue ball to contact three cushions before the second object ball. This means that the player can often leave the cue ball in a position where the opponent has no easy shot, forcing the opponent to attempt a difficult three-cushion contact or to play safe.

Leaving difficult positions for the opponent is a key strategy in 3-cushion. When a player cannot score, they should aim to leave the cue ball and the object balls in positions that are difficult for the opponent. This can be achieved by: (1) Leaving the cue ball far from both object balls, (2) Leaving the object balls close together (making it difficult for the opponent to achieve three cushion contacts), (3) Leaving the object balls near the short rails (where the diamond system is less effective), (4) Leaving the cue ball in a position where the opponent has no natural angle to the first object ball.

When to play safe vs. attempt scoring is a critical decision in 3-cushion. The general rule is: if the probability of scoring is low (e.g., less than 30%), it is better to play safe. If the probability of scoring is high (e.g., greater than 60%), the player should attempt to score. The decision also depends on the score — if the player is behind, they may need to take more risks to catch up; if they are ahead, they can afford to play more conservatively.

Common game patterns and solutions in 3-cushion include: (1) The "natural" pattern (both object balls near the same long rail), (2) The "cross-table" pattern (object balls on opposite short rails), (3) The "end-rail" pattern (object balls near the short rail at the end of the table), (4) The "cluster" pattern (all three balls close together), (5) The "spread" pattern (all three balls far apart). Each pattern has a preferred solution, and experienced players can quickly identify the pattern and choose the best approach.

The concept of "inning management" is important in 3-cushion strategy. An inning is a single turn at the table. A player should aim to maximize the number of points scored per inning while minimizing the number of innings where no points are scored. This means that the player should focus on making safe, high-percentage shots rather than attempting difficult, low-percentage shots. Over the course of a game, the player who makes fewer mistakes will typically win.

Position play in 3-cushion requires the player to think two or three shots ahead. After scoring a point, the player should aim to leave the cue ball in a position where they can score again on the next shot. This requires careful control of the cue ball's speed and direction, as well as an understanding of how the cue ball will behave after contacting the cushions and the object balls.
